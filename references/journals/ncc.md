## Writing Preferences

### Journal Fit

- EVERY paper must have explicit, clear climate change relevance.
- The work must appeal to the climate science community broadly, not just specialists.
- Writing must be accessible to scientists across climate-related disciplines.
- Emphasize climate change relevance throughout: Introduction, Results, and Discussion.

### Style Preferences

- Prefer clear, direct prose that connects findings to climate change.
- Use cautious but confident language: `suggest`, `indicate`, `reveal` for interpretations.
- Past tense for methods and results; present tense for general knowledge and climate context.
- Figures should be self-contained with comprehensive captions.
- Define technical terms on first use; avoid unexplained acronyms.
- Emphasize the climate change angle in every major section.

### Avoid

- Papers without clear climate change relevance
- Excessive jargon inaccessible to the broader climate community
- `novel`, `groundbreaking`, `revolutionary`, or similar self-promotional adjectives
- Discussion that does not address broader climate implications
- Narrow conclusions with no climate change significance
- Over-technical methods in the main text

## Quality Checks

### Before Submission

- [ ] The abstract is ~150 words, has no references, and highlights climate change relevance.
- [ ] The manuscript has explicit, clear climate change relevance throughout.
- [ ] The work is understandable by climate scientists outside the specific sub-field.
- [ ] Figures (4-6) are high quality with self-contained captions.
- [ ] The Discussion addresses broader climate change implications.
- [ ] A data availability statement is present.
- [ ] Author contributions are included.
- [ ] Competing interests are declared.

### Tense and Tone Checks

- Methods are described in the past tense.
- Results are reported in the past tense.
- Discussion emphasizes climate change implications, often in present tense.
- Conclusion should connect findings to the broader climate change narrative.

## Section Guidance

### Abstract
- **Purpose:** State the key finding and its climate change significance in ~150 words.
- **Expected content:** Climate change context, approach, core result, climate significance.
- **Common pitfalls:** Missing climate change angle, too much technical detail, vague significance, exceeding 150 words.

### Introduction
- **Purpose:** Establish climate change context, identify the gap, and state the contribution.
- **Expected content:** Climate change motivation, literature gap in the context of climate science, contribution, climate significance.
- **Common pitfalls:** Weak or missing climate change framing, too narrow for the broad climate audience, contribution not connected to climate change.

### Results
- **Purpose:** Present findings with clear connection to climate change questions.
- **Expected content:** Quantitative findings organized as a coherent narrative, explicit connections to climate processes, figure references.
- **Common pitfalls:** Results disconnected from climate change framing, excessive technical detail, losing the climate narrative.

### Discussion
- **Purpose:** Interpret findings in the context of climate change science and articulate broader implications.
- **Expected content:** Climate change interpretation, comparison to prior work, broader climate implications, limitations, future directions under climate change.
- **Common pitfalls:** Missing broader climate implications, repeating results, narrow interpretation, no connection to climate projections or policy.

### Methods
- **Purpose:** Provide sufficient detail for reproducibility, concise and focused.
- **Expected content:** Data sources, analytical procedures, statistical methods, climate model configurations if applicable.
- **Common pitfalls:** Excessive detail for main text (move to Supplementary), insufficient detail for reproducibility.

### Conclusion
- **Purpose:** State the key finding and its significance for climate change understanding.
- **Expected content:** Key result, climate change significance, future outlook.
- **Common pitfalls:** Inflated claims, introducing new evidence, conclusions without climate change relevance.
