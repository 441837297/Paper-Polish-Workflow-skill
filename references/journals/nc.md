## Writing Preferences

### Journal Fit

- The work must represent a significant advance of interest to a broad scientific audience.
- Writing must be accessible to scientists outside the specific sub-field. Avoid excessive jargon.
- Show why the work matters beyond the immediate discipline.
- Prefer clear narrative and broad significance over narrow technical depth.

### Style Preferences

- Prefer clear, direct prose accessible to non-specialists.
- Use cautious but confident language: `suggest`, `indicate`, `reveal` for interpretations; `demonstrate`, `show` for robust findings.
- Past tense for methods and results; present tense for general knowledge and broader implications.
- Figures should be self-contained with comprehensive captions that can be understood without reading the main text.
- Define technical terms on first use; avoid unexplained acronyms.

### Avoid

- Excessive jargon inaccessible to scientists outside the sub-field
- `novel`, `groundbreaking`, `revolutionary`, or similar self-promotional adjectives
- Discussion paragraphs that repeat results without broader interpretation
- Over-technical methods in the main text (move to Methods or Supplementary)
- Narrow conclusions with no broader significance

## Quality Checks

### Before Submission

- [ ] The abstract is ~150 words, has no references, and is accessible to a broad audience.
- [ ] The manuscript is understandable by a scientist outside the specific sub-field.
- [ ] Figures (4-6) are high quality with self-contained captions.
- [ ] The narrative has a clear, logical flow from problem to conclusion.
- [ ] Technical jargon is defined and kept to a minimum.
- [ ] A data availability statement is present.
- [ ] Author contributions are included.
- [ ] Competing interests are declared.

### Tense and Tone Checks

- Methods are described in the past tense.
- Results are reported in the past tense.
- Discussion and broader implications can shift to present tense.
- Conclusion should emphasize significance and broader impact.

## Section Guidance

### Abstract
- **Purpose:** State the key finding and its significance in ~150 words, accessible to a broad audience.
- **Expected content:** Brief context accessible to non-specialists, approach, core result, broad significance.
- **Common pitfalls:** Too much technical detail, jargon inaccessible to non-specialists, missing significance, exceeding 150 words.

### Introduction
- **Purpose:** Establish broad context, identify the gap, and state the contribution in an accessible manner.
- **Expected content:** Broad scientific motivation accessible to non-specialists, literature gap, contribution, significance.
- **Common pitfalls:** Too narrow or technical for the general reader, weak significance, contribution buried in jargon.

### Results
- **Purpose:** Present findings as a clear, logical narrative.
- **Expected content:** Quantitative findings with interpretation, organized as a coherent story, figure references with clear explanations.
- **Common pitfalls:** Presenting results without narrative structure, excessive technical detail, losing the reader in jargon.

### Discussion
- **Purpose:** Interpret findings and articulate broader implications.
- **Expected content:** Interpretation in broader context, comparison to prior work, significance beyond the immediate field, limitations.
- **Common pitfalls:** Repeating results, narrow interpretation, missing broader implications.

### Methods
- **Purpose:** Provide sufficient detail for reproducibility, placed after main text or in Supplementary.
- **Expected content:** Data sources, analytical procedures, statistical methods, model configurations.
- **Common pitfalls:** Insufficient detail for reproducibility, excessive detail that belongs in Supplementary.

### Conclusion
- **Purpose:** State the key finding and its broad significance concisely.
- **Expected content:** Key result, broader significance, future outlook.
- **Common pitfalls:** Inflated claims, introducing new evidence, narrow conclusions with no broader impact.
