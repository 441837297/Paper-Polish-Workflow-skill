## Writing Preferences

### Journal Fit

- Keep a clear ocean-science perspective throughout the paper.
- Emphasize rigorous, quantitative, and well-referenced analysis.
- Show relevance to ocean sciences broadly; interdisciplinary work is welcome.
- JGR-Oceans has a broader scope than JPO, encompassing physical oceanography, biogeochemistry, marine geology, and interdisciplinary ocean research.

### Style Preferences

- Prefer precise, quantitative claims supported by measurements or model output.
- Use cautious verbs such as `suggest`, `indicate`, or `demonstrate` unless strong causal evidence is available.
- Past tense for methods and results; present tense for general knowledge and established theory.
- Make transition sentences functional and logically connect findings across disciplines when applicable.
- Include proper units, coordinates, and error estimates throughout.

### Avoid

- Speculative claims without quantitative support
- Overgeneralization beyond the study region without justification
- `novel`, `groundbreaking`, `revolutionary`, or similar self-promotional adjectives
- Discussion paragraphs that repeat results without interpretation
- Vague references to "previous studies" without specific citations

## Quality Checks

### Before Submission

- [ ] The abstract fits the JGR-Oceans length constraint (~250 words).
- [ ] The manuscript addresses an ocean-science question with rigorous methodology.
- [ ] Data and methods are described clearly enough for reproducibility.
- [ ] Figures have proper units, coordinates, and legends.
- [ ] Claims are calibrated and supported by evidence.
- [ ] A data availability statement compliant with AGU data policy is present.
- [ ] Author contributions are included.

### Tense and Tone Checks

- Methods are usually described in the past tense.
- Results are usually reported in the past tense.
- Discussion can shift to present tense when interpreting broader implications.
- Conclusion should stay measured; avoid claiming universal validity beyond the study domain.

## Section Guidance

### Abstract
- **Purpose:** State the research problem, approach, key findings, and broader significance compactly.
- **Expected content:** Context, gap, method, core result, interdisciplinary or broader significance.
- **Common pitfalls:** Too much background, missing evidence, vague contribution, no clear significance statement.

### Introduction
- **Purpose:** Establish the oceanographic or interdisciplinary context, identify the knowledge gap, and state the contribution.
- **Expected content:** Ocean science motivation, literature review identifying the gap, specific contribution, paper roadmap.
- **Common pitfalls:** Generic ocean framing without specific research question, weak gap identification, contribution stated too late.

### Data and Methods
- **Purpose:** Describe data sources, analytical or numerical methods, and quality control clearly enough for evaluation.
- **Expected content:** Data provenance (observations, models, reanalysis), processing methods, quality control procedures, statistical approaches, model configuration if applicable.
- **Common pitfalls:** Missing quality control documentation, insufficient methodological detail, unclear data processing steps.

### Results
- **Purpose:** Present quantitative findings with interpretation and spatial/temporal patterns.
- **Expected content:** Main findings, spatial and temporal patterns, quantitative comparisons, figure/table references with proper units and coordinates.
- **Common pitfalls:** Reporting values without interpretation, overclaiming without statistical support, hiding negative or mixed findings.

### Discussion
- **Purpose:** Interpret findings, compare with prior studies, and address broader implications.
- **Expected content:** Mechanism-level or process-level interpretation, comparison to prior studies, broader implications, limitations.
- **Common pitfalls:** Repeating results without interpretation, overstating generalizability, skipping mechanistic or interdisciplinary context.

### Conclusion
- **Purpose:** Close with the main takeaways, bounded contribution, and realistic future directions.
- **Expected content:** Short recap, key findings, significance, future work.
- **Common pitfalls:** Inflated claims beyond study domain, introducing new evidence, overly broad statements without support.
